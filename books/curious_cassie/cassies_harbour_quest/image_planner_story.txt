*(If you want to discover gravity with Cassie, look for "Curious Cassie's Beach Ride Quest: A Celebration of Sir Isaac Newton's Discoveries!")*

"Exactly right," Mum said. "When a boat sits on water, gravity tries to pull it down to the bottom‚Äîjust like gravity pulls you down when you jump. But there's another force working against gravity. It's called **buoyancy**, and buoyancy pushes things UP!"

"Perfect description! When gravity wins, objects sink. When buoyancy wins, they float. It's a tug-of-war between two forces."

"What decides who wins?" Caleb asked, completely absorbed.

Mum pulled out a large clear container and filled it with water right to the brim. She held up Cassie's empty juice bottle.

"Is this bottle heavy or light?"

"Super light!" both children agreed.

"See what happened? The bottle pushed some water out of the way‚Äîthat's called displacement. The water that was pushed aside creates the buoyant force pushing up on the bottle. Because this empty bottle is so light, gravity isn't pulling very hard downward. Buoyancy wins the battle‚Äîthe bottle floats!"

"Can I try something?" Cassie asked, an idea forming.

She removed the bottle and carefully filled it with water until it was heavy. She lowered it into the container and... *glug glug glug*... down it went, settling on the bottom!

"They can! Anemones have thousands of tiny stinging cells in their tentacles‚Äîlike microscopic harpoons‚Äîto catch small prey that floats by. But remarkably, there's one type of fish that's completely immune to the stings. Do you know which one?"

"Exactly right! Clownfish have a special slimy coating on their skin that protects them from anemone stings. They live right inside the anemone's tentacles, where predators can't reach them. And in return, the clownfish helps keep the anemone clean and brings it food scraps!"

"So they help each other?" Cassie asked, fascinated by this arrangement.

"Precisely. Scientists call it a **symbiotic relationship**‚Äîwhen two different species help each other survive. The ocean is absolutely full of these remarkable partnerships!"

Can you think of any other animal partnerships you know about?

Cassie noticed something peculiar. Some of the rocks appeared to be moving! No, wait‚Äîthose weren't rocks at all...

"And look at this one's colouring," Dad said, pointing to a small crab half-hidden among green and brown seaweed. "See how it's greenish-brown? That's **camouflage**‚Äîcoloration and patterns that help an animal blend into its surroundings so predators can't easily spot it."

Once Cassie understood what to look for, she spotted camouflaged creatures everywhere! A tiny fish the exact colour of sand. A nearly transparent shrimp. A snail whose shell looked identical to the pebbles surrounding it. It was like one of those hidden picture puzzles, except real and alive!

"It's amazing how invisible they can be!" she exclaimed.

"Scientists who study harbours‚Äîlike Professor Emma Johnston‚Äîhave discovered that rock pools are like tiny, complete ecosystems," Mum said. "An **ecosystem** is a community where all the living things interact and depend on each other for survival."

"What do you mean by 'depend on each other'?" Cassie asked.

"Think about it like this: tiny algae and seaweed grow on the rocks, using sunlight and nutrients from the water. Small snails and limpets eat the algae. Crabs eat the snails. Bigger fish might eat the crabs. And when any creature dies, bacteria break down its body, releasing nutrients back into the water that help more algae grow. Everything is connected in a circle‚Äîscientists call it a food web!"

Cassie gazed at the pool with completely new eyes. It wasn't just a puddle trapped between rocks‚Äîit was an entire world with its own food chains, neighbourhoods, dramas, and survival stories!

"Professor Johnston's research proved that even in busy harbours‚Äîwith boats and activity‚Äîthese little ecosystems can adapt and survive," Dad added. "She showed that marine life is remarkably resilient. But it still needs our help to stay healthy."

"That's why we never leave rubbish near water," Mum emphasized. "And why we look but don't touch the rock pool creatures. Every single animal here has an important role in keeping the ecosystem balanced!"

Caleb had been very quiet, watching something intently. "Mum," he whispered carefully. "The jelly thing is doing something!"

Everyone gathered around. A small wave had just washed over the rock pool, bringing fresh seawater and oxygen. And slowly, magically, the sea anemone began to open. Soft tentacles unfurled like delicate flower petals, swaying gracefully in the gentle current.

**Science Words We Learned:**

- **Adaptation**: Special features that help an animal survive in its particular environment
- **Invertebrate**: An animal without a backbone or internal skeleton
- **Camouflage**: Colours or patterns that help an animal blend into its surroundings and hide from predators
- **Symbiotic relationship**: When two different species help each other survive
- **Ecosystem**: A community where all living things interact and depend on each other
- **Food web**: The interconnected feeding relationships in an ecosystem

**Create Your Own Mini Ecosystem** 

‚ö†Ô∏è **IMPORTANT: Ask an adult to help with this experiment!**

**You'll need:** A large glass jar with a lid, aquarium gravel or small pebbles, aged tap water (leave tap water sitting for 24 hours so chlorine evaporates), small aquatic plants from a pet shop, a few small pond snails or aquatic insects from an aquarium store

**Instructions:**
1. Wash your hands before and after handling aquarium materials
2. Add a layer of clean pebbles (about 2-3 cm deep) to your jar
3. Carefully pour in aged water until the jar is about three-quarters full
4. Gently add your aquatic plants, pressing their roots into the gravel
5. Add your snails or insects
6. Place your mini ecosystem in a spot with indirect sunlight (not direct sun, which gets too hot)
7. Observe over several weeks!

**What to watch for:**
- Plants producing oxygen bubbles (photosynthesis!)
- Snails eating algae that grows on the glass and plants
- Waste from animals providing nutrients for plants
- The whole system balancing itself!

- **NEVER take creatures from rock pools or natural habitats**‚Äîthis harms wild populations
- Only use animals from pet shops that are bred for aquariums
- **NEVER release pets or aquarium creatures into natural waterways**‚Äîthey can harm local ecosystems
- If you observe rock pools in nature, look but don't touch, and never remove anything
